We spend a lot of time imagining the future. Flying cars. Hoverboards. Robot servants. Cities on Mars.

But the technologies that change civilisation are rarely the ones that look revolutionary when they arrive.

The printing press looked like a faster way to make books. Radio looked like a way to hear distant voices. Smartphones looked like phones with extra features.

None of them stayed that way. The future always arrives. It just never looks like the posters.

The Pattern

SAME STORY.
DIFFERENT
CENTURY.

You have now studied six technologies across five centuries. Each one was different. Each one followed the same structure.

Every technology increased human capability. Every technology disrupted an existing authority — the Church, the state, the press, the broadcaster, the gatekeeper. Every technology created new freedoms and new dangers, simultaneously, from the same change.

None were utopian. None were dystopian. Every one was both — depending on what you valued and where you stood when it arrived.

The question was never whether the technology was good or bad. The question was always: what was gained, what was lost, and who gets to decide whether the trade was worth it?

And there is one more pattern. Look at where the attention was in each case — and where the disruption actually landed.

The disruption is never where the attention is. The hoverboard was the wrong question. The smartphone was the right answer — people were watching for flying cars while a device arrived that restructured politics, commerce, attention, and knowledge. The pattern is portable: GPS looked like navigation, the disruption landed in spatial memory. Streaming looked like convenience, the disruption landed in ownership. Where does AI land?

THE DISRUPTION IS NEVER WHERE THE ATTENTION IS.

Six technologies. Five of them are history — the consequences are known, the pattern is visible, the disruption has been measured. The sixth is different. AI is the first case in this instrument where history has not finished yet. You are standing in the middle of it. You cannot judge it the way you judged the printing press. The printing press is done. This one is not. Where is the attention right now — and where is the disruption actually landing?

And: who gets to decide whether the trade was worth it? Who actually decides — and are those the same people?

The Case Still Being Written

You have now seen the pattern six times. Here is a seventh case. It is not history. It is happening now. Apply the framework yourself.

Neurotechnology · 2023–present

In January 2023, Duke University professor Nita Farahany told the World Economic Forum in Davos that more than 5,000 companies around the world were already using neurotechnology to monitor employee brainwaves — detecting fatigue, concentration, and emotional state. She described wearable devices, like earphones, that could read brain activity in real time. She argued the technology could improve workplace safety, reduce accidents, and increase productivity.

She also presented a short film imagining the near future: an employee worries that her employer's neurotechnology has detected her "amorous feelings" for a colleague. She receives a performance bonus based on her "brain metrics." Later, the government subpoenas employee brainwave data to identify co-conspirators in a fraud case. She is innocent — but her earbuds recorded that she had been secretly working on a startup with the accused. She removes them, shaking.

"What I don't want the reaction to be is: let's ban this." — Nita Farahany, Davos, January 2023

Where is the attention? Where might the disruption land?
Productivity. Safety. Fatigue prevention. Accident reduction. Worker wellbeing. Cognitive privacy. Freedom of thought. The boundary between mind and institution. The question of whether thought itself can become data.

Every previous technology in this instrument made something newly visible, measurable, or shareable — interpretation, images, voices, behaviour, information. Neurotechnology would make thought measurable. Whether this is simply the next step in the pattern — or a fundamentally different kind of threshold — is the question you now have to answer. The question the pattern leaves you with: is there anything about a person that should never become data?
