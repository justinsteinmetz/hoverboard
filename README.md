

# Dude, Where's My Hoverboard?
### The future always arrives. It just never looks like the posters.

**Live:** https://justinsteinmetz.github.io/HoverboardHistory/

**Strand:** The Technology and Society Trilogy · Historical and analytical instrument · Run second

---

## What it is

A historical instrument in six cases and one test. Each case examines a technology that transformed society — not by doing what people expected, but by disrupting something they weren't watching. The cases span five centuries. The pattern is the same in every one.

The instrument is not about technological history. It is about how societies misread technological change while it is happening. The hoverboard is not the subject. It is the symbol — the wrong prediction, the cinematic future that captured attention while the real transformation arrived in a different form.

The smartphone was the right answer to the wrong question. So was the printing press. So was every technology in this instrument. **The disruption is never where the attention is.**

---

## The central design principle

There are two common framings for technological disruption:

1. *Was this technology good or bad?* — assigns a verdict
2. *What did this technology gain and lose?* — identifies a trade-off

This instrument uses neither. It uses a third framing:

3. *Where was the attention, and where did the disruption actually land?*

This is a different kind of question. It is not evaluative. It is analytical. It does not ask students to judge the printing press — it asks them to observe that the attention was on books and the disruption landed on authority. Having observed that pattern across six cases, students are equipped to ask the same question about any technology — including the ones they are currently inside.

The shift from question 2 to question 3 is the instrument's central pedagogical move. Students who leave asking *where was the attention, where did the disruption land?* have acquired a transferable analytical habit. Students who leave asking *was this good or bad?* have not.

## The six cases

| Case | Technology | Where attention went | Where disruption landed |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Printing Press (c.1450) | Books — faster, cheaper | Authority over interpretation |
| 2 | Photography (c.1839) | Objective evidence | Trust in images as proof |
| 3 | Radio (c.1920) | Distant voices, communication | Centralisation of the nation's ear |
| 4 | Television (c.1950) | Entertainment, mass culture | The structure of public discourse |
| 5 | Social Media (2004–) | Connection, universal publication | Verification and shared reality |
| 6 | Artificial Intelligence (2020–) | Generation — infinite, cheap | Confidence in origin |

### Case 1 — The Printing Press
Gutenberg's press looked like a faster way to make books. Within fifty years there were printing presses in every major European city and an estimated 20 million books in circulation — more than had existed in all of Europe's history. Martin Luther's 95 Theses reached every corner of Germany within two weeks and every corner of Europe within two months. The Church could no longer control what people read or how they interpreted what they read. The result was not simply religious freedom — the Thirty Years' War killed an estimated eight million people. The same technology produced both.

### Case 2 — Photography
The daguerreotype was announced in 1839. The assumption was immediate: the camera is objective. Within three decades, Alexander Gardner was rearranging bodies at Gettysburg for better compositions. Stalin was removing Trotsky from official photographs. The Nazis were producing photographic exhibitions associating Jewish people with degeneracy. The camera never lied. But the gap between what photographs showed and what they meant became the space in which the twentieth century's most sophisticated propaganda operated.

### Case 3 — Radio
Roosevelt's Fireside Chats created an intimacy between a president and citizens that no previous medium had made possible. The Nazis produced the Volksempfänger — a cheap receiver distributed across Germany — and by 1939 70% of German households owned one. The same technology that allowed Roosevelt to reassure a frightened public allowed Hitler to inflame one. The qualities that made radio democratic made it the perfect instrument of centralised control. They were the same qualities.

### Case 4 — Television
Television brought the Vietnam War and the civil rights movement into American living rooms. What had been deniable became visible. Neil Postman argued in 1985 that television's preference for images over argument, entertainment over complexity, and the immediate over the considered was systematically degrading public discourse. The same vividness that exposed injustice made politics into spectacle. The same medium.

### Case 5 — Social Media
The Arab Spring of 2010–2012 was organised partly on social media. Wael Ghonim, a key organiser of the Tahrir Square protests: *"We thought we would have freedom of expression and we would fight the regime. We used it against the regime, and then the regime used it against us."* The same platforms spread genocide-inciting content in Myanmar, coordinated the January 6th attack on the US Capitol, and radicalised young men through algorithmic recommendation systems. The editors who had previously decided what was published were not only gatekeepers of power. They were also, imperfectly, filters of falsehood. When they disappeared, both things went.

### Case 6 — Artificial Intelligence
The Clarkesworld literary magazine closed submissions in 2023 after being flooded with AI-generated stories. A Japanese literary prize shortlisted an AI-assisted novel whose judges could not, on rereading, identify the AI passages. Deepfakes became cheap enough to produce on a consumer laptop. The previous five technologies disrupted the authority that controlled information production. AI does something different: it removes the cost of production entirely. The question is no longer who controls what gets published. Everything gets published. The question is: when everything can be generated, how do you know what was made by a person — and whether it matters?

## The post-pattern test case: Neurotechnology

After the six cases, students encounter a seventh — but not as a historical case. As a test.

In January 2023, Duke University professor Nita Farahany told the World Economic Forum in Davos that more than 5,000 companies worldwide were already monitoring employee brainwaves to detect fatigue, concentration, and emotional state. She described wearable devices — like earphones — that can read brain activity in real time. She presented a short film imagining the near future: an employee receives a performance bonus based on her brain metrics; the government then subpoenas employee brainwave data to identify fraud co-conspirators. An innocent employee removes her earbuds, shaking.

Farahany's stated purpose: *"What I don't want the reaction to be is: let's ban this."*

Students are asked to apply the framework themselves — without stance buttons, without guidance. Where is the attention? Where might the disruption land? The question the framework then asks: *is there anything about a person that should never become data?*

This case is not history. It is happening. Students cannot judge it with the same confidence they judged the printing press. The consequences are not yet known. The pattern is visible. The answer is not.

## How it works

**Intro screen** — the promise/reality structure, reframed. Not *we expected X and got Y* (which implies disappointment) but *people were watching for X while Y arrived and changed everything*. The smartphone was the right answer to the wrong question — people were looking for flying cars while a device arrived that restructured politics, commerce, attention, and knowledge.

**For each case:** the case in full — narrative, primary source quotation, gain/loss table — followed by three stances (something gained, something lost, or both from the same change) and a free-text reflection field.

**Pattern screen** — two tables. The first shows gain and loss across all six technologies. The second — the key addition — shows where the attention went and where the disruption actually landed. This is the instrument's core analytical gift: a framework students can apply to any technology, including ones the instrument doesn't cover.

**The Farahany test** — presented after the pattern is named, before the discussion questions. No stance options. Students apply the framework without scaffolding.

**Nothing is stored.** No login required. Approximately 20–30 minutes.

## The portable framework

The most important thing students leave with is not knowledge of six historical cases. It is the ability to ask one question about any technology:

*Where is the attention — and where is the disruption actually landing?*

This question is generative. Students who have the question can apply it immediately:

- GPS looked like navigation. The disruption landed in spatial memory and local knowledge.
- Streaming looked like convenience. The disruption landed in ownership and the economics of creative work.
- Smartphones looked like phones with extra features. The disruption landed in politics, commerce, attention, and knowledge.

The pattern table does not close with AI. It opens with AI. The final discussion question asks: where is the attention on AI right now — and where is the disruption actually landing? Are those the same place?

## What the instrument refuses to do

**Refuses the progress narrative.** The instrument does not argue that technological change is good. But it also does not argue that it is bad. Every case presents genuine gains and genuine losses simultaneously. The printing press produced mass literacy and religious war. The same technology. Students who arrive with a position on whether technology is good or bad find that position dissolved by the structure before they have a chance to settle into it.

**Refuses the disappointment framing.** The title — Dude, Where's My Hoverboard? — is not about technological failure. The smartphone is not the consolation prize for flying cars. It is the right answer to a question nobody knew they were asking. The instrument insists on this from the first screen. The future arrived. It just never looks like the posters.

**Refuses premature pattern recognition.** The pattern table arrives after six cases, not before. Students who are given the attention/disruption framework before the cases apply it mechanically. Students who encounter the cases first and then see the table have the experience of recognising something they were already thinking — which is more durable and more honest.

**Refuses the verdict on the final case.** The AI row in the second pattern table asks students to identify where the attention is and where the disruption is landing. The instrument does not fill this in. It asks students to. And then it asks: is this the next step in the pattern — or a fundamentally different kind of threshold?

## Classroom notes

**Time:** 20–30 minutes for the instrument. Discussion is open-ended.

**Grade:** Grades 11–12. KMK Themenfeld 8 — Science and Technology.

**Run second in the trilogy** — after *Who Pays for Progress?*, before *What Are You For?*. Students who have already asked *gained by whom, lost by whom* bring a sharper political awareness to the historical pattern. The cases in *Hoverboard* become more legible when students already know that disruption has political consequences.

**The most productive moment** is the second pattern table — attention vs. disruption across six technologies. Ask students to name additional examples before you discuss the AI case. GPS. Streaming. Smartphones. Social media algorithms. The pattern becomes portable in the room, not just on the screen.

**On the Farahany test:** do not explain it before students encounter it. The test works because students have just been told they understand the pattern. Then they are asked to apply it to a case that is genuinely unsettling. The employer detecting romantic feelings; the government subpoenaing thought-adjacent data. Let that land before facilitating discussion.

**On the closing question:** six of them are in the instrument. The most important is the last: *where is the attention on AI right now — and where is the disruption actually landing?* This is the question that connects *Hoverboard* to *What Are You For?*. Students who have been trained to ask it are ready for the existential version.

**Success is not the correct answer to the pattern.** Students who leave saying *the disruption is never where the attention is* and can generate their own examples have acquired something durable. Students who leave able to correctly label historical cases but cannot generate examples have not.
